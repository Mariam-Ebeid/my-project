The evolution of human sexuality is one of the most debated scientific issues, and while Darwin's theory of evolution is the most famous in this field, it has not settled the matter. Undoubtedly, the search for the unknown always arouses curiosity and the love of knowledge in humans in general, and researchers and scientists in particular. From this standpoint, many researchers in various fields, such as medicine, sociology, and linguistics, are passionate about researching the origin and evolution of humans, whether in themselves or in comparison with other beings. Driven by this desire, Dr. Ahmed El Batrawy, an anatomist, embarked on presenting his vision of human evolution to the Arabic reader, using the science of anatomy to compare humans with other creatures, especially modern humans' close relatives, the great apes. The author then discusses the inhabitants of Egypt from prehistoric times to modern times, and the brilliance of ancient Egyptians in the field of anatomy and the extent of their contribution to its development.

The Chemistry Among Arabs
Rouhi Al-Khalidi
Science
20,285 words
The remarkable achievements of modern chemistry would not have been possible without passing through previous stages undertaken by a large battalion of dedicated scientists whose goals were unified to benefit humanity and its prosperity. Our discussion of chemistry leads us to mention the efforts of Arab scientists such as "Jabir ibn Hayyan" (whom the West dubbed as the father of chemistry), "Al-Farabi," and "Ibn Sina," among others. They steered chemistry away from the myths of sorcery and magic, and the fantastical notions spread by some ancient philosophers — like their belief in the existence of a "philosopher's stone" that could transform base metals into gold — which associated it with magic and astrology. They authored numerous substantial books in which they documented their experiments and opinions, many of which coincided with modern discoveries and theories in chemistry, and sometimes even preceded them. Calculating the Universe in Numbers
Ian Stewart
Science
20,285 words
Starting with the Babylonians who integrated mathematics into their study of astronomy, the author Ian Stewart traces the evolution of human understanding of the universe. After centuries of misunderstanding the nature of the Earth as the center of the cosmos, Copernicus introduced his revolutionary idea of the sun's centrality, stating that the Earth is just an ordinary planet orbiting it. Kepler further developed this cognitive structure and formulated the laws of planetary motion, which later led Newton to devise his theory of gravity. Centuries later, slight deviations in Mercury's motion inspired Einstein to formulate the theory of general relativity, and the concept of the singularity led to the proposal of other additions such as cosmic inflation, dark matter, and dark energy. But do these theories suffice to explain the structure of the universe? Does dark matter truly exist?
